For this to be a perfect square every exponent \( a_i q \) must be even.

Consider each exponent \( a_i q \). Since \( a_i \) and \( q \) are integers \( a_i q \) is even if and only if at least one of \( a_i \) or \( q \) is even. Because \( q \) is prime it is even if and only if \( q = 2 \). Thus for each \( i \) either \( a_i \) is even or \( q = 2 \).

Problem:
Define a domino to be an ordered pair of distinct positive integers. A proper sequence of dominoes is a list of distinct dominoes in which the first coordinate of each pair after the first equals the second coordinate of the immediately preceding pair, and in which $(i, j)$ and $(j, i)$ do not both appear for any $i$ and $j$. Let $D_{n}$ be the set of all dominoes whose coordinates are no larger than $n$. Find the length of the longest proper sequence of dominoes that can be formed using the dominoes of $D_{n}$.

Solve Final Channel:
We model the problem using graph theory. Each domino $(a, b)$ with $a \neq b$ is represented as a directed edge from vertex $a$ to vertex $b$ in a complete graph with $n$ vertices. A proper sequence corresponds to a trail in this directed graph where no two edges are reverses of each other, meaning the graph contains exactly one directed edge between each pair of vertices—a tournament.

The length of the longest proper sequence equals the length of the longest trail in any tournament on $n$ vertices. For a directed graph to admit an Eulerian trail (a trail using every edge exactly once), it must be strongly connected, and all vertices except two must have equal in-degree and out-degree. Specifically, one vertex may have out-degree exceeding in-degree by $1$ (the start), and one vertex may have in-degree exceeding out-degree by $1$ (the end).

In any tournament, the sum of out-degrees is $\frac{n(n-1)}{2}$, as each undirected edge contributes one directed edge. For a vertex $v$, let $d^+(v)$ denote its out-degree and $d^-(v)$ its in-degree. Then $d^+(v) + d^-(v) = n - 1$ for all $v$.

When $n$ is odd, a regular tournament exists where $d^+(v) = \frac{n-1}{2}$ for all $v$. In such a tournament, all vertices have equal in-degree and out-degree. It is strongly connected because, for any two vertices $u$ and $v$, suppose no directed path exists from $u$ to $v$; then the vertex set partitions into non-empty sets $A$ and $B$ with all edges from $A$ to $B$. Let $|A| = k$. Each vertex in $A$ has out-degree at least $|B| = n - k$, but $\frac{n-1}{2} \geq n - k$ implies $k \geq \frac{n+1}{2}$. Each vertex in $B$ has out-degree at most $k - 1$, but $\frac{n-1}{2} \leq k - 1$ implies $k \geq \frac{n+3}{2}$, contradicting $k \leq n$. Thus, the tournament is strongly connected and Eulerian, allowing a trail of length $\frac{n(n-1)}{2}$.

When $n$ is even, no tournament can be Eulerian. Each vertex must have out-degree either $\frac{n}{2} - 1$ or $\frac{n}{2}$, resulting in $\frac{n}{2}$ vertices with $d^+(v) - d^-(v) = 1$ and $\frac{n}{2}$ vertices with $d^-(v) - d^+(v) = 1$. To form an Eulerian trail, we need exactly one vertex with excess $+1$ and one with excess $-1$. Removing an edge from a vertex with excess $+1$ to one with excess $-1$ reduces both excesses by $1$, but since we cannot alter the tournament, we must exclude edges. Each exclusion reduces the total edge count by $1$ and adjusts the excesses. Exactly $\frac{n}{2} - 1$ edges must be excluded to achieve the required imbalance, yielding a maximum trail length of $\frac{n(n-1)}{2} - \left( \frac{n}{2} - 1 \right) = \frac{n(n-2)}{2} + 1$.

Therefore, the length of the longest proper sequence is $\frac{n(n-1)}{2}$ if $n$ is odd and $\frac{n(n-2)}{2} + 1$ if $n$ is even.
