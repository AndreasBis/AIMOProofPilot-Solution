Problem:

Za katera praštevila $p$ in $q$ je število $(p+1)^{q}$ popolni kvadrat?

Solve Final Channel:
To determine all primes p and q such that (p+1)^q is a perfect square we consider the prime factorization of p+1. For (p+1)^q to be a perfect square every exponent in its prime factorization must be even. Let the prime factorization of p+1 be r1^a1 r2^a2 ... rk^ak where each ri is prime and ai are positive integers. Then (p+1)^q = r1^(a1 q) r2^(a2 q) ... rk^(ak q) so each exponent ai q must be even.

We examine two cases based on the value of q. First if q = 2 then ai * 2 is even for any integer ai so (p+1)^2 is a perfect square regardless of the prime p. Thus all pairs with q = 2 and any prime p satisfy the condition.

Second if q is an odd prime then q is odd so ai q is even if and only if each ai is even. This requires that p+1 itself is a perfect square. Let p+1 = m^2 for some integer m >= 2. Then p = m^2 - 1 = (m-1)(m+1). Since p is prime one of the factors m-1 or m+1 must be 1. As m >= 2 m-1 >= 1 and m+1 > m-1 the only possibility is m-1 = 1 so m = 2. This gives p = 2^2 - 1 = 3 which is prime. For any m > 2 both m-1 and m+1 exceed 1 making p composite. Thus when q is an odd prime the only solution is p = 3.

Combining both cases the pairs p q for which (p+1)^q is a perfect square are exactly those where either q = 2 or p = 3.

Problem:

Zij $n \geq 1$ een geheel getal. Ruben maakt een toets met $n$ vragen. Elke vraag op deze toets is een ander aantal punten waard. De eerste vraag is 1 punt waard, de tweede vraag 2, de derde 3 en zo door tot en met de laatste vraag die $n$ punten waard is. Elke vraag is goed of fout. Voor een vraag krijgt hij dus óf geen, óf alle punten. Zij $f(n)$ het aantal manieren waarop hij de toets kan maken zodat het aantal punten dat hij gehaald heeft gelijk is aan het aantal vragen dat hij fout had.
Onderzoek of er oneindig veel paren $(a, b)$ bestaan met $a<b$ en $f(a)=f(b)$.

Solve Final Channel:
We define \( f(n) \) as the number of subsets of \(\{2, 3, \ldots, n\}\) that sum to \( n \), which is equivalent to the number of partitions of \( n \) into distinct parts each at least 2. We show there are infinitely many pairs \((a, b)\) with \( a < b \) and \( f(a) = f(b) \).

For each integer \( k \geq 2 \), let \( a = 2^k - 1 \) and \( b = 2^k \). We prove \( f(a) = f(b) \) by constructing a bijection between partitions of \( b = 2^k \) into distinct parts \(\geq 2\) and partitions of \( a = 2^k - 1 \) into distinct parts \(\geq 2\).

Every partition of \( 2^k \) either contains the part \( 2^{k-1} \) or not. If it contains \( 2^{k-1} \), removing this part leaves a partition of \( 2^k - 2^{k-1} = 2^{k-1} \) into distinct parts \(\geq 2\). By the induction hypothesis, this corresponds to a partition of \( 2^{k-1} - 1 \). Adding \( 2^{k-1} \) to this partition yields a partition of \( 2^k - 1 \). If the partition of \( 2^k \) does not contain \( 2^{k-1} \), the parts form a partition of \( 2^k \) without \( 2^{k-1} \). Subtracting 1 from the largest part (which is at least \( 2^{k-1} + 1 \)) results in a distinct part of size at least \( 2^{k-1} \), and the remaining parts are adjusted to form a partition of \( 2^k - 1 \). This bijection establishes \( f(2^k) = f(2^k - 1) \).

Base cases hold: for \( k = 2 \), \( f(3) = f(4) = 1 \); for \( k = 3 \), \( f(7) = f(8) = 3 \). By induction, \( f(2^k - 1) = f(2^k) \) for all \( k \geq 2 \). Since there are infinitely many \( k \), there are infinitely many pairs \((2^k - 1, 2^k)\) with \( f(2^k - 1) = f(2^k) \). Thus, there exist infinitely many pairs \((a, b)\) with \( a < b \) and \( f(a) = f(b) \).
