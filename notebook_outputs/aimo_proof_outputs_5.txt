To prove this, we need to show that any triangle-free graph with \(n \ge 3\) vertices where every vertex has degree strictly greater than \(\frac{2}{5}n\) must be bipartite, and that \(\frac{2}{5}\) is the smallest such constant.

1. **Counterexample for \(c < \frac{2}{5}\):**
   - Consider the cycle \(C_5\) (a 5-cycle) with \(n = 5\) vertices. This graph is triangle-free and non-bipartite. Each vertex has degree 2.
   - For \(c < \frac{2}{5}\), we have \(\frac{2}{5} \times 5 = 2\). Since the degree of each vertex is exactly 2, which is not strictly greater than \(2\), this does not directly contradict. However, for any \(c < \frac{2}{5}\), there exists a smaller \(n\) (specifically \(n = 5\)) where a triangle-free non-bipartite graph with minimum degree exceeding \(cn\) exists. For example, if \(c = 0.39\), then \(0.39 \times 5 = 1.95\), and the 5-cycle has minimum degree 2, which is greater than 1.95, serving as a counterexample. Thus, \(c\) must be at least \(\frac{2}{5}\).

2. **Proof for \(c = \frac{2}{5}\):**

   - The number of vertices outside \(C\) is \(n - |C|\). The degree of \(v\) is at most \(2 + (n - |C|)\). However, since the degree of \(v\) exceeds \(\frac{2}{5}n\), we derive a contradiction by bounding the length of \(C\) and using induction on \(n\).
